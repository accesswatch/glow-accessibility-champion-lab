
**WCAG 2.1 AA vs. WCAG 2.2 AA: A Quick Overview**

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are designed to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities. The guidelines are periodically updated to address new challenges and technologies. The most notable recent update is the transition from WCAG 2.1 to WCAG 2.2. Here’s a summary of the differences and actionable steps for a campus content owner:

### Key Differences

**1. Introduction of New Success Criteria:**

- WCAG 2.2 introduces new success criteria aimed at improving accessibility in various areas, especially for users with cognitive and learning disabilities, and users of mobile devices.

- **2.2.2 Pause, Stop, Hide (Enhanced):** While WCAG 2.1 includes this under Level A as 2.2.2, WCAG 2.2 emphasizes flexible mechanisms for the user to pause, stop, or hide content.

- **2.4.11 Focus Appearance (Minimum)**
  - WCAG 2.2 introduces a focus appearance requirement to help users better track where their keyboard or input focus is on a page.

- **2.5.7 Dragging Movements:**
  - Actions that require dragging should also provide an alternative mechanism. This is particularly helpful for users with motor impairments.

- **3.2.6 Consistent Help:**
  - Accessing help should be consistent across pages, improving user experience especially for visitors who need assistance.

- **3.3.7 Accessible Authentication:**
  - Offers alternative methods of authentication to accommodate users who might have difficulty with typical authentication methods (e.g., CAPTCHA).

**2. Enhanced Criteria:**

- Some success criteria from WCAG 2.1 have been refined for better clarity and effectiveness in WCAG 2.2.

### What to Do Differently This Week

1. **Audit Current Content:**
   - Review existing web and digital content for compliance with the new WCAG 2.2 criteria. Focus on areas such as navigability, focus states, and alternatives for drag-and-drop interfaces.

2. **Update Authentication Methods:**
   - If your campus platforms use authentication, review and adapt these processes to offer accessible alternatives that meet the new requirements.

3. **Improve Navigational Focus:**
   - Check all interactive elements on your site to ensure keyboard focus indicators are visible and meet the new focus appearance requirements.

4. **Consistent Help Features:**
   - Ensure that help features (like FAQs, contact support, and guidance) are available and consistent across all web pages.

5. **Test with Real Users:**
   - Engage users with disabilities to test key areas of your site and provide feedback on how well it meets the new guidelines, focusing on the newly introduced criteria.

6. **Training and Resource Allocation:**
   - Allocate time and resources for your team to learn about WCAG 2.2 and its implications. Consider training sessions or workshops to enhance understanding and compliance.

By reviewing and updating your current content with these steps, you can ensure your campus meets the latest accessibility standards and offers an inclusive digital environment for all users.

## Alt text purpose

Prompt:

```text
Explain why AI can help draft alt text options but cannot decide image purpose without human context.
```

Output:

AI has made significant strides in helping to draft alt text options for images, primarily through advanced image recognition technologies and language models. Here's how AI can contribute to this task and why human input remains essential for understanding image purpose:

1. **Image Recognition**: AI can identify objects, scenes, and even text within images, which allows it to generate descriptive alt text options. By analyzing the visual content, AI can suggest possible elements to describe, such as the appearance of people, animals, or objects, and any visible actions.

2. **Language Models**: Modern AI systems use sophisticated language models to convert visual data into coherent text descriptions. These models can create grammatically correct and semantically meaningful sentences that represent the content of an image.

However, the following limitations highlight why AI cannot completely determine an image's purpose without human context:

1. **Understanding Intent**: The purpose of an image often depends on the context in which it is used, including the surrounding text, the subject of the article or page, and the intended audience. AI lacks the ability to fully grasp these nuances and the broader narrative they contribute to.

2. **Nuances and Contextual Cues**: While AI can identify what is visually present in an image, it often struggles to comprehend the subtleties and context beyond its immediate scope. For example, the significance of an image might lie not just in its content but in what it symbolizes or how it relates to surrounding information.

3. **Cultural and Emotional Resonance**: Images can convey complex cultural and emotional meanings that AI systems have difficulty interpreting accurately. Human interpretation is needed to understand these deeper levels of meaning and to articulate them into alt text that aligns with the image's purpose.

4. **Accessibility Considerations**: Alt text serves as an accessibility tool for individuals who are visually impaired. Therefore, the alt text needs to succinctly capture what is critical about the image's role in the content. AI may generate descriptive text but miss the specific focus or takeaway that human insight would prioritize.

To ensure that alt text fits the intent and accessibility requirements, collaboration between AI-generated suggestions and human oversight is critical. Humans provide the context and intent that refine and finalize the AI's initial drafts into meaningful alt text that truly meets user needs.

Creating a human-review checklist for AI-assisted accessibility guidance is essential to ensure that the AI's recommendations are accurate, relevant, and truly enhance accessibility. Here's a comprehensive checklist that could be used by reviewers:

### General Principles

1. **Understandability**
   - Confirm that the AI's suggestions are communicated clearly and avoid technical jargon that may be confusing.
   - Ensure the guidance respects cultural differences and is inclusive.

2. **Accuracy**
   - Verify that AI-generated accessibility recommendations align with established accessibility standards (e.g., WCAG, ADA).
   - Cross-check the AI's advice with the latest best practices in accessibility.

3. **Relevance**
   - Ensure the suggestions are pertinent to the specific context of the application, platform, or content.
   - Determine if the guidance addresses the correct accessibility needs for the target audience.

4. **Feasibility**
   - Assess whether the recommendations are practical and achievable within the given constraints (e.g., budget, technology, resources).

5. **Clarity of Implementation Steps**
   - Verify that the AI provides clear, actionable steps to implement its accessibility advice.

### Specific Elements

1. **Text and Content**
   - Check if AI suggests appropriate use of headings, lists, links, and clear content layout.
   - Verify correct semantic structure and alternative text for images and multimedia.

2. **Color and Contrast**
   - Ensure the guidance on color choices and contrast ratios follows accessibility guidelines.
   - Recommend colorblind-friendly designs if necessary.

3. **Navigation and Structure**
   - Evaluate suggestions for improving keyboard navigation and reducing reliance on mouse input.
   - Check advice for logical and consistent structure throughout the content.

4. **Interactive Elements**
   - Confirm appropriate guidance for making forms, buttons, and controls accessible.
   - Ensure the inclusion of visible focus states and clear instruction.

5. **Media Accessibility**
   - Validate suggestions for providing captions, transcripts, and audio descriptions for multimedia content.

6. **Assistive Technology Compatibility**
   - Cross-verify that the AI's guidance supports the seamless use of screen readers and other assistive technologies.

1. **User Testing**
   - Ensure recommendations include suggestions for user testing with individuals who have disabilities.
   - Confirm there is a feedback loop to refine AI recommendations based on user experience.

- **Report Findings**

- Ensure the review process itself remains up to date with new technological advances and accessibility standards.
- Foster a collaborative approach among AI developers, accessibility experts, and end-users in refining AI-assisted guidance.
