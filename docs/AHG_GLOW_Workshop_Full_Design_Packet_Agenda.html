Accessibility Agents in Action

A Hands-On GLOW Workshop for Human-Centered Accessibility Workflows

Subtitle: Helping Everyone Become an Accessibility Champion

Full Workshop Design Packet for Review

Prepared for Howard Kramer and the Accessing Higher Ground Program Committee

Prepared by Jeff Bishop, IT Accessibility Consultant, University of Arizona

May 2026

Workshop promise

Participants will not need to be AI scientists, AI developers, or programmers. They will be met where they are, guided through practical accessibility problems, and supported in designing repeatable workflows that help their partners learn, practice, and GLOW.

G - Ground the work in a real accessibility problem.
L - Learn what people need to understand.
O - Organize a repeatable workflow.
W - Walk forward as accessibility champions.

Cover Letter to Howard Kramer

Hi Howard,

Thank you again for the committee's continued consideration of my proposal. I have rebuilt the session as a full-day, highly interactive workshop with seven hours of working time, plus a one-hour lunch break.

The revised title I recommend is: Accessibility Agents in Action: A Hands-On GLOW Workshop for Human-Centered Accessibility Workflows. The subtitle is: Helping Everyone Become an Accessibility Champion.

At the heart of the workshop is a simple idea: accessibility cannot belong only to accessibility specialists. Our role is not simply to fix every document, web page, course shell, or communication ourselves. Our role is to help partners build confidence, develop good habits, understand their responsibility in the accessibility journey, and become accessibility champions. In other words, we need to teach our partners to fish.

This version uses GLOW as the primary demonstration and hands-on practice environment. It is intentionally approachable for a broad AHG audience. Participants do not need to be AI scientists, AI developers, or programmers. They will be met where they are and guided through practical labs, online forms or guided walkthroughs, shared galleries of participant work, peer review, and a capstone activity.

I have also included a companion concept called the GLOW Lab Hub: an accessible online space where participants can submit work through guided forms, see each other's examples in shared galleries, provide peer feedback, and leave with reusable artifacts. This would make the workshop more magical, repeatable, consumable, and learnable while modeling the same accessibility values the workshop teaches.

Most importantly, the workshop is designed around the theme: everyone has a role in the accessibility journey, and with the right guidance, everyone can GLOW.

Best,

Jeff Bishop
IT Accessibility Consultant
University of Arizona

The corrected schedule below runs from 8:30 AM to 4:30 PM with lunch from 12:15 PM to 1:15 PM. The scheduled workshop block contains seven hours outside lunch, with the two short breaks included in that block. This keeps the event within a true one-day format while preserving interaction, labs, peer review, and capstone sharing.

8:30 - 8:50: Welcome, Orientation, and Accessibility Journey Check-In

Purpose: Establish a collaborative, accessible learning environment and center the idea that everyone has a role in the accessibility journey.

Activity: Participants respond to three prompts: What kind of accessibility work do you do? Where do partners get stuck? What would change if more people saw themselves as accessibility champions?

Output: A shared list of real barriers and partner needs that will be referenced throughout the day.

8:50 - 9:25: What Problem Are We Solving?

Purpose: Start with the problem, not the tool.

Activity: Participants identify a recurring accessibility problem from their organization and distinguish the surface problem from the deeper capacity-building problem.

Output: A problem statement that begins with: The accessibility problem we need to solve is...

9:25 - 10:00: From Fixing Everything to Teaching Partners to Fish

Purpose: Reframe accessibility specialists as guides, coaches, and capacity builders.

Activity: Groups review partner requests such as "Can you just fix this PDF?" and practice turning them into coaching opportunities.

Output: Coaching responses that teach the partner what to do differently next time.

10:00 - 10:10: Break

Purpose: Provide a short pause while keeping the day within the seven-hour workshop block.

Activity: Participants stretch, check messages, and return ready for the responsible AI boundary exercise.

Output: Participants return ready to continue.

10:10 - 10:45: Human-Centered AI: Helpful, Risky, or Human Required?

Purpose: Establish practical boundaries for responsible AI use in accessibility work.

Activity: Participants classify tasks as helpful for AI, risky without review, or human required, then discuss what each classification means in practice.

Output: An AI boundary map that participants can use when designing workflows.

10:45 - 11:20: Accessibility Agents in Plain Language

Purpose: Introduce the agent concept without jargon or developer assumptions.

Activity: Participants use the formula Role + Task + Trusted Guidance + Output Format + Human Review to describe a simple accessibility helper.

Output: A first accessibility agent concept that is understandable to non-technical partners.

11:20 - 12:15: GLOW Lab 1 - Helping Partners Improve Accessible Communications

Purpose: Use GLOW to improve a realistic communication while teaching partners the accessibility pattern.

Activity: Participants revise sample content, produce a review checklist, and share outputs through the GLOW Lab Hub or guided worksheet.

Output: Revised accessible communication and a human-review checklist.

12:15 - 1:15: Lunch

Purpose: Provide a full lunch break without reducing the seven-hour workshop structure.

Activity: Optional reflection prompt: What is one accessibility task in your organization where people need more confidence, not just more correction?

Output: Participants return with ideas for afternoon workflow design.

1:15 - 2:05: GLOW Lab 2 - Alt Text, Image Purpose, and Human Judgment

Purpose: Use AI to support image description decisions without pretending AI can determine purpose alone.

Activity: Groups analyze image scenarios, draft guidance with GLOW, and identify human verification steps.

Output: Image-description coaching checklist.

2:05 - 2:55: GLOW Lab 3 - Document, Slide, and Course Remediation Planning

Purpose: Turn overwhelming remediation problems into manageable coaching workflows.

Activity: Groups choose a track and create a prioritized remediation plan and content-owner coaching message.

Output: Remediation coaching template.

2:55 - 3:05: Break

Purpose: Provide a short pause before participants begin building their own workflows.

Activity: Participants review their lab notes and select a real partner-facing problem for the design studio.

Output: Participants are ready to design their own accessibility champion workflow.

3:05 - 3:50: Accessibility Champion Studio

Purpose: Participants design a workflow that teaches, not just fixes.

Activity: Individuals or small groups complete the Accessibility Champion Workflow Worksheet, focusing on what the partner should learn and what human review must verify.

Output: Draft reusable workflow, GLOW-ready prompt, checklist, or agent concept.

3:50 - 4:15: Peer Review, Scaling Path, and Final Refinement

Purpose: Make workflows more trustworthy, teachable, reusable, and ready to share.

Activity: Participants exchange workflows, use the peer review checklist, and identify whether the workflow should become a GLOW prompt, GLOW skill, checklist, template, training resource, agent concept, or institutional practice.

Output: Revised workflow with a clear next form and implementation path.

4:15 - 4:30: Capstone Share-Out and 30-Day GLOW Action Plan

Purpose: Celebrate the work, reinforce peer learning, and convert workshop energy into action.

Activity: Selected participants share final artifacts, then everyone completes a 30-day action plan naming one workflow, one partner or team, one human-review safeguard, and one way to help others GLOW.

Output: A take-home artifact and practical next-step commitment.

10. GLOW Lab Hub: Online Forms, Shared Galleries, and Guided Walkthroughs

To make the workshop more magical, visible, and repeatable, the session can include a companion online experience called the GLOW Lab Hub. This would be a simple, accessible web-based workshop space that guides participants through each exercise, captures their submissions, displays shared work in galleries, supports peer feedback, and exports artifacts after the session.

Why Build a GLOW Lab Hub?

  • It models the same accessibility values the workshop teaches.

  • It lets participants see each other's work as it is submitted.

  • It turns worksheets into guided online walkthroughs.

  • It gives the facilitator examples to highlight in real time.

  • It creates a living library of accessibility champion workflows.

  • It makes the experience feel collaborative, fun, glowing, and practical.

  • It creates reusable output that can become prompts, checklists, GLOW skills, or open-source examples.

Minimum Viable Feature Set

Feature Purpose
Workshop landing page Provides agenda, instructions, links, and accessibility information.
Guided form for each exercise Prompts participants step-by-step so they do not need to guess what to submit.
Shared gallery Displays submissions as accessible cards organized by lab or workflow type.
Peer feedback form Allows participants to provide structured feedback to another group.
Facilitator dashboard Shows submissions, common themes, and examples worth highlighting.
Export function Exports selected artifacts to Markdown, Word, JSON, or a reusable workflow library.
Optional GLOW skill pathway Allows selected workflows to become future GLOW skills or Accessibility Agents examples.

Accessibility Requirements for the GLOW Lab Hub

  • Keyboard accessible from start to finish.

  • Screen-reader friendly headings, labels, instructions, and error messages.

  • No visual-only status indicators.

  • Clear focus order and visible focus indication.

  • Forms with labels, instructions, examples, and accessible validation.

  • Shared gallery cards with headings and meaningful link text.

  • Plain-language instructions.

  • Short URLs in addition to QR codes.

  • Exportable content for participants who need offline access.

  • Accessible fallback worksheets in Word or HTML.

  • No time-limited interactions that prevent participation.

Recommended Build Approach

The GLOW Lab Hub could be implemented as a small purpose-built WordPress plugin, a static web application with form handling, or a lightweight application connected to the Accessibility Agents ecosystem. The first version does not need to be complex. It should be accessible, stable, easy to use, and capable of capturing and displaying participant work in a structured way.

11. Exercise and Lab Facilitation Guide

Exercise 1: Accessibility Journey Check-In

Time: 20 minutes

Purpose: Help participants locate themselves in the accessibility journey and identify where partners need support.

Setup: Use a GLOW Lab Hub form or paper worksheet. Ask participants to answer individually, then invite brief sharing.

Participant instructions:

  1. What kind of accessibility work do you do?

  2. Where do your partners most often get stuck?

  3. What would change if more people in your organization saw themselves as accessibility champions?

Participant output: A set of participant-identified accessibility challenges and partner needs.

Debrief questions:

  • What patterns do we hear across the room?

  • Where are people asking specialists to fix instead of helping them learn?

  • Where could a guided workflow reduce fear or confusion?

Exercise 2: What Problem Are We Solving?

Time: 35 minutes

Purpose: Train participants to start with the accessibility problem instead of the tool.

Setup: Use the Problem Statement Canvas. Encourage participants to choose a real challenge from their organization.

Participant instructions:

  1. Name the surface problem.

  2. Name the deeper capacity problem.

  3. Identify who needs to learn something.

  4. Describe what success would look like if the problem were solved in a repeatable way.

Participant output: A clear problem statement.

Debrief questions:

  • Did the problem change when you looked beneath the surface?

  • Who needs to become more capable?

  • What would make the solution repeatable?

Exercise 3: Fix It for Me vs. Teach Me to Improve It

Time: 40 minutes

Purpose: Help participants practice turning urgent repair requests into coaching opportunities.

Setup: Provide short partner-request scenarios. Groups rewrite each response in a way that preserves kindness while building capacity.

Participant instructions:

  1. Read the request.

  2. Identify the immediate accessibility need.

  3. Identify what the partner needs to learn.

  4. Draft a response that helps now and teaches for next time.

Participant output: A set of coaching responses.

Debrief questions:

  • How do we avoid sounding punitive?

  • How do we keep urgency without creating dependency?

  • What language helps partners feel capable?

Exercise 4: Helpful, Risky, or Human Required?

Time: 40 minutes

Purpose: Build judgment about responsible AI use in accessibility work.

Setup: Create cards or online form items with AI-supported accessibility tasks.

Participant instructions:

  1. Classify each task as helpful for AI, risky without review, or human required.

  2. Explain why.

  3. Identify the safeguard needed.

Participant output: An AI boundary map.

Debrief questions:

  • Which tasks surprised you?

  • Where can AI help people get unstuck?

  • Where must human expertise remain in control?

Exercise 5: Accessibility Agent Formula

Time: 45 minutes

Purpose: Give participants a simple, non-technical way to design an accessibility agent.

Setup: Introduce the formula: Role + Task + Trusted Guidance + Output Format + Human Review.

Participant instructions:

  1. Choose a partner need.

  2. Define the role.

  3. Define the task.

  4. Name the trusted guidance.

  5. Define the output.

  6. Define human review.

Participant output: A first accessibility agent concept.

Debrief questions:

  • Is the agent narrow enough?

  • Does it teach?

  • Does it preserve human responsibility?

GLOW Lab 1: Helping Partners Improve Accessible Communications

Time: 60 minutes

Purpose: Use GLOW to improve a realistic communication while teaching participants how to coach partners toward better accessibility habits.

Scenario: Participants work with a sample event announcement, campus communication, newsletter item, or course notice that includes dense language, unclear links, missing headings, vague access information, and unclear next steps.

Sample starting point: Join us for our exciting event next Friday. Click here to register. If you need anything, contact our office. We will talk about new services, updates, and important things everyone needs to know. More information will be sent later.

Suggested GLOW prompt or workflow instruction:

You are an accessibility communication coach. Review this announcement for clarity, structure, meaningful link text, plain language, inclusive access language, and missing information. Provide a revised version, a list of the most important accessibility improvements, and a human-review checklist. Explain the pattern so the content owner can improve the next announcement.

Participant output:

  • Revised communication

  • Top accessibility issues

  • Teaching note for the partner

  • Human-review checklist

Peer review or debrief questions:

  • What improved?

  • What still needs human review?

  • What pattern would help the partner next time?

  • How could this become a reusable GLOW workflow?

GLOW Lab 2: Alt Text, Image Purpose, and Human Judgment

Time: 55 minutes

Purpose: Show how AI can support image-description decisions while keeping purpose, context, and meaning in human hands.

Scenario: Groups review image-description scenarios involving decorative images, functional images, informative photos, charts, and complex diagrams.

Sample starting point: Scenario: A campus newsletter includes a photo of a student using a tactile graphics display in a STEM lab. The surrounding article is about inclusive STEM access and student independence.

Suggested GLOW prompt or workflow instruction:

You are an alt text coaching assistant. Help the content creator decide the purpose of the image, what information matters, what can be omitted, whether short alt text or a longer description is needed, and what a human must verify. Draft possible alt text options and explain the decision process.

Participant output:

  • Image purpose statement

  • Draft alt text or description guidance

  • Human verification questions

  • Image-description coaching checklist

Peer review or debrief questions:

  • Is the purpose clear?

  • Does the draft include only relevant details?

  • What would a human need to verify?

  • Would this teach a content creator how to make a better decision next time?

GLOW Lab 3: Document, Slide, and Course Content Remediation Planning

Time: 55 minutes

Purpose: Turn overwhelming remediation problems into manageable coaching workflows.

Scenario: Participants choose a track: Word document remediation, slide deck accessibility, PDF remediation planning, course content cleanup, accessible syllabus review, web page issue explanation, or digital communication improvement.

Sample starting point: Scenario: A faculty member provides a 35-page course handout with inconsistent headings, scanned images of text, unclear links, several tables, and no clear document title. The faculty member asks, "Can you just make this accessible by tomorrow?"

Suggested GLOW prompt or workflow instruction:

You are a document remediation coach. Create a prioritized remediation plan for this content. Identify likely barriers, questions for the content owner, what can be improved quickly, what requires human inspection, and what the content owner should learn to avoid the same problems next time.

Participant output:

  • Plain-language barrier summary

  • Prioritized remediation plan

  • Questions for the content owner

  • Coaching message

  • Human-review checklist

Peer review or debrief questions:

  • Is the plan realistic?

  • Does it prioritize the most important barriers?

  • Does it teach the content owner?

  • What human review is missing?

Accessibility Champion Studio: Design a Workflow That Teaches

Time: 50 minutes

Purpose: Participants design their own workflow around a real need from their organization.

Scenario: Participants select a partner group, identify a repeated accessibility challenge, and design a workflow that helps that partner become more capable.

Sample starting point: Example workflow: Faculty Email Accessibility Coach. The workflow helps faculty write clearer, more accessible emails with meaningful links, clear headings or structure, plain language, and appropriate accommodation information.

Suggested GLOW prompt or workflow instruction:

Use the Accessibility Champion Workflow Worksheet. Create a workflow that helps a partner learn and improve, not merely submit work to be fixed. Include the problem, partner responsibility, GLOW/AI support, trusted guidance, output, and human review.

Participant output:

  • Draft accessibility champion workflow

  • Teaching goal

  • GLOW-ready prompt

  • Human-review safeguard

Peer review or debrief questions:

  • Does the workflow teach?

  • Is the partner responsibility clear?

  • Is the output reusable?

  • How does it help someone become a champion?

Peer Review Lab: Make the Workflow More Trustworthy, Teachable, and Reusable

Time: 40 minutes

Purpose: Use peer feedback to improve each workflow before the capstone.

Scenario: Groups exchange workflows and use a structured peer review checklist.

Sample starting point: Peer reviewers look for clarity, trustworthy guidance, human review, teachability, reusability, and accessibility champion potential.

Suggested GLOW prompt or workflow instruction:

Review this accessibility workflow as a supportive peer. Identify one strength, one question, one risk, one missing human-review step, and one suggestion to make it more reusable and teachable.

Participant output:

  • Peer feedback

  • Revised workflow

Peer review or debrief questions:

  • What became clearer after feedback?

  • What safeguard was added?

  • How did the workflow become more teachable?

13. Facilitator Materials and Accessibility Checklist

Materials Needed

  • GLOW access and backup prompts.

  • GLOW Lab Hub or accessible online forms.

  • Short URLs for all online activities, not only QR codes.

  • Accessible Word or HTML worksheets as a backup.

  • Sample communication text.

  • Image-description scenarios.

  • Document, slide, PDF, course content, and web remediation scenarios.

  • Peer review checklist.

  • Timer or timekeeper.

  • Microphone plan for group sharing.

  • Accessible slide deck or HTML agenda.

  • Facilitator dashboard for submissions.

  • Export plan for participant artifacts.

Accessibility Checklist for the Workshop Experience

  • Provide instructions in accessible formats before and during the workshop.

  • Use headings, lists, labels, and clear structure in all materials.

  • Ensure all forms are keyboard accessible and screen-reader friendly.

  • Avoid requiring visual-only QR code access; provide short URLs.

  • Ensure shared galleries are navigable by heading and meaningful links.

  • Read or summarize visual information presented on screen.

  • Allow individual, pair, and group participation options.

  • Avoid time pressure that prevents thoughtful accessibility review.

  • Provide offline backup worksheets.

  • Make peer review supportive, not evaluative or punitive.

  • Preserve privacy by allowing participants to anonymize sensitive examples.

Facilitator Tone

The tone should be practical, encouraging, and capacity-building. Participants should feel that accessibility is achievable, not mysterious. AI should be framed as a tool that can help people learn and practice, not as a replacement for expertise or accountability. The facilitator should repeatedly return to the workshop promise: meet people where they are, teach partners to fish, and help everyone GLOW.

The core workshop does not require programming. However, participants or institutions with technical capacity may choose to extend the workflows into reusable technical assets.

Checklist or template A non-technical artifact that can be used in training or daily work.
Institutional workflow A documented practice for a unit, department, or accessibility champion network.
GitHub example A shareable open-source version for community improvement.
Visual Studio Code and GitHub Copilot Optional tools for technical participants who want to formalize agent instructions or supporting code.

Important framing

Technical tools are extension paths, not entry requirements. The workshop is designed so every participant can contribute meaningfully whether they are a developer, accessibility specialist, instructor, content creator, program manager, or campus partner.

15. Short Version for AHG Program Review

Accessibility Agents in Action: A Hands-On GLOW Workshop for Human-Centered Accessibility Workflows is a full-day interactive workshop designed for accessibility professionals, disability services staff, instructional designers, document remediation specialists, content creators, and campus technology leaders. The workshop introduces Accessibility Agents as structured AI-supported workflows that help people analyze barriers, explain accessibility issues, create remediation guidance, and improve content while preserving human judgment and accountability.

The session is built around the belief that accessibility must be a shared journey. Accessibility specialists provide expertise and guidance, but sustainable accessibility requires helping partners understand their responsibility, build confidence, and become accessibility champions. The workshop uses the GLOW framework: Ground the work in a real accessibility problem, Learn what people need to understand, Organize a repeatable workflow, and Walk forward as accessibility champions.

GLOW will serve as the primary demonstration and practice environment. Participants will engage in hands-on labs, small-group exercises, peer review, online guided walkthroughs, shared galleries of participant work, and capstone development. Activities will include improving accessible communications, developing alt text guidance, planning document remediation, explaining accessibility issues in plain language, and designing reusable workflows for real accessibility challenges.

No programming experience is required. Participants will learn a simple model: Accessibility Agent = Role + Task + Trusted Guidance + Output Format + Human Review. By the end of the workshop, each participant or group will leave with a practical artifact such as a GLOW-ready prompt, accessibility workflow, remediation checklist, human-review guide, or accessibility champion action plan.
