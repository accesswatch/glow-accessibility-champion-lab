## Session Notes

Module 1 drew a clear line between what AI models are and what they are not.
The technical vocabulary — AI, machine learning, deep learning, neural
networks, large language models, foundation models, generative AI — can feel
overwhelming, but the organizing idea underneath is simple: each term is a
narrowing of scope. Generative AI sits at the end of a long chain of
increasingly specific design choices, not at the beginning.

What stood out most was the framing around hallucination. AI outputs are
statistically plausible, not factually verified. That is a structural
property of the technology, not a bug that gets patched. For accessibility
work — which involves legal standards, policy obligations, accommodation
decisions, and real people's access to education — that distinction matters
enormously.

## Questions

- When a campus partner uses a tool like ChatGPT or Claude to draft
  accessibility guidance, how do we help them understand that the output is
  a starting point, not an answer?
- How do we build human review into a workflow without making the workflow
  so slow that partners abandon it?
- Foundation models are trained on general data. How much does the relative
  scarcity of high-quality disability and accessibility content in training
  data affect the reliability of accessibility-specific outputs?
- At what point does a helpful AI prompt become an accessibility specialist
  decision that a partner should not be making alone?

## Capstone Connections

The vocabulary from Module 1 is the foundation for explaining why GLOW needs
a human review gate. If I cannot explain to a campus partner what an LLM
actually is and why it produces plausible-sounding text that is not always
accurate, I cannot explain why they should not trust every accessibility
suggestion it generates.

The concept map for this module makes the point visually: the AI models are
not the center. Human Review is the hub. GLOW Workflows and Campus Partners
are the destination. That framing should run through every module deliverable.

The capstone thesis — that we need human-reviewed AI workflows to help campus
partners become accessibility champions — depends entirely on understanding
what Module 1 establishes: AI generates, humans verify.
